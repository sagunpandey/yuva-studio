# Solo Gaming: Playing Alone Together

Solo board gaming has exploded in popularity over the last decade. What was once an afterthought — a tacked-on solo mode in the rulebook's appendix — has become a first-class design goal for many publishers.

## Why Solo Gaming Works

Playing alone doesn't mean playing without challenge. The best solo games pit you against an **automa** — an automated opponent system that reacts, adapts, and punishes mistakes without requiring a second player.

Games like **Wingspan**, **Arkham Horror: The Card Game**, and **Friday** are specifically designed to be deeply satisfying in solo play.

## The Automa System

An automa simulates an opponent using a simplified decision tree:
- Draw a card from the automa deck
- Execute the action it specifies
- Score points according to its own rubric

This elegance means solo play moves fast while still feeling competitive.

## Puzzle vs. Narrative Solo

There are two broad solo experiences:

**Puzzle-style** — You're racing against a clock or score threshold. *Gaia Project solo*, *Race for the Galaxy*.

**Narrative-style** — You're unfolding a story. *Sleeping Gods*, *Gloomhaven: Jaws of the Lion*.

Both are valid, and many games blend the two.

## Design Tips for Solo Modes

1. **Don't just add a bot** — design the solo experience from the ground up if possible
2. **Difficulty scaling matters** — solo players want to feel progression
3. **Short session lengths** — solo gamers often play in 30–60 minute windows
4. **Clear win/loss conditions** — ambiguity kills satisfaction in solo play

---

Solo gaming proves that the best opponent is sometimes the game itself.
